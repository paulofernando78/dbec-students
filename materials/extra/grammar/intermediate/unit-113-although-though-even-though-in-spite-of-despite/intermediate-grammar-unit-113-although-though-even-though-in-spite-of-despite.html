Unit 113 • Although / though / even tough - in spite of / despite


Although and even though have a similar meaning to in spite of and despite.


We use all of these words to say that a thing is surprising, unusual or unexpected in relation to another thing. This is called concession.


A


Study this example situation.



Last year Paul and Sarah had a vacation by the sea. It rained a lot, but they enjoyed it.

holiday.


You can say:

Although it rained a lot, they enjoyed it. Embora tenha chovido muito, eles gostaram. / Apesar de ter chovido muito, eles gostaram.

or

In spite of / Despite the rain, they enjoyed it. Apesar da chuva, eles gostaram.


B


After although we use a subject + verb:


• Although it rained a lot, we enjoyed our vacation vacation.

• Although he's much older than the others, he won the race.

• Although she's a millionaire, she lives in a very small flat.

• I didn't get the job although I had the necessary qualifications.


Compare the meaning of although and because.


• We went out although it was raining heavily.

• We didn't go out because it was raining heavily.


C


After in spite of or despite, we use a:


noun

• In spite of the rain, we enjoyed our holiday.


pronoun (this/that/what etc.)

• She wasn't well, but in spite of this she continued working.

• In spite of what I said yesterday, I still love you.


verb + -ing

• In spite of / Despite being much older than the others, he won the race.

• She lives in a very small flat in spite of / despite being a millionaire.

• I didn't get the job in spite of having the necessary qualifications.


Despite is the same as in spite of. We say in spite of, but despite:

• She wasn't well, but despite this she continued working.


You can say in spite of / despite the fact (that) subject + verb:

• I didn't get the job in spite of the fact (that) I had the necessary qualifications.

• I didn't get the job despite the fact (that) I had the necessary qualifications.


Compare in spite of and because of:

• We went out in spite of the rain. (or… despite the rain.)

• We didn't go out because of the rain.


D


Compare although and in spite of / despite:

• Although the traffic was bad, we arrived on time.

• In spite of the traffic, we arrived on time.

• I couldn't sleep although I was very tired.

• I couldn't sleep in spite of being very tired.

• I couldn't sleep despite being very tired.


E


Though is the same as although:

• I didn't get the job though I had the necessary qualifications.


In spoken English, we often use though at the end of a sentence.

• The house isn't so nice. I like the garden though. (=but I like the garden)

• I see them every day. I've never spoken to them though. (=but I've never spoken to them)


Even though (but not 'even' alone) is a stronger form of although.

Even though I was really tired, I couldn't sleep.
